# Grind Settings Determinants Framework

> **Important:** Grind size is never set by brew method alone. Five factors determine the optimal grind — brew method sets the *starting point*, but coffee characteristics (processing, origin, roast, variety) must shift that starting point up or down. Ignoring these factors is why recipes fail.

## The Core Principle

Grind size controls **extraction speed** and **water-coffee contact time**:
- **Finer grind** → more surface area → faster extraction → need shorter contact time or you'll over-extract
- **Coarser grind** → less surface area → slower extraction → need longer contact time or you'll under-extract

This is why a single brew method (e.g., V60) can need grind settings ranging from medium-fine to medium-coarse depending on the coffee.

## The Five Grind Determinants

Grind adjustments stack. Start with your **brew method base**, then apply **processing adjustment**, then **origin/altitude**, then **roast level**, then **variety**. The final number is your starting point — always taste and refine.

### Determinant 1: Brew Method (The Base Setting)

This sets your foundation. Each method has a typical grind range based on its extraction mechanics:

| Brew Method | Base Grind | Why |
|-------------|-----------|-----|
| Turkish | Extra Fine (powder) | Full immersion, no filter resistance, maximum extraction time |
| Espresso | Fine | High pressure, very fast extraction, small window |
| AeroPress | Medium-fine to Medium | Versatile; pressure accelerates extraction |
| V60 / Pour-Over | Medium-fine | Conical, fast flow, needs some resistance |
| Siphon | Medium-fine | Full immersion + cloth filter, medium contact time |
| Kalita / Flat-Bottom | Medium | Flat bed, three-hole drainage, balanced |
| Chemex | Medium-coarse | Thick paper filters slow drainage; coarser prevents over-extraction |
| French Press | Coarse | Full immersion, long steep, no filter resistance |
| Cold Brew | Extra Coarse | 12-24hr immersion, room temp, very slow extraction |
| Moka Pot | Medium-fine | Pressure-based, medium-fast extraction |

> **Micron ranges (approximate):** Turkish 40-220μm | Espresso 180-380μm | V60/AeroPress 400-700μm | Chemex/FP 700-1000μm | Cold Brew 1000-1400μm

### Determinant 2: Processing Method (Critical — Affects Solubility)

This is the most commonly missed factor. **Processing changes solubility** — the same grind size will extract differently depending on how the coffee was processed.

| Process | Solubility | Grind Adjustment | Why |
|---------|-----------|-----------------|-----|
| **Washed** | Standard | Base setting | Clean seeds, even extraction, predictable |
| **Natural** | Higher (20-30% more soluble) | **Coarser** (+1-3 clicks) | Fruit mucilage surrounding seed = faster extraction; coarser grind slows it down |
| **Honey (white/yellow)** | Slightly higher | Base to +1 click | Light mucilage contact, minimal solubility increase |
| **Honey (red/black)** | Moderately higher | Base to +1-2 clicks | Significant mucilage, more sugar conversion |
| **Anaerobic / Experimental** | Much higher | **Coarser** (+2-4 clicks) | Fermentation breaks cell walls, extracts very fast |
| **Carbonic Maceration** | Higher | Coarser (+1-3 clicks) | Wine-like, alcohol-adjacent; coarser prevents over-extraction |
| **Thermal Shock / Frozen** | Higher | Coarser (+1-3 clicks) | Processing disrupts cell structure, faster dissolution |

**The rule:** If processing adds solubility, grind coarser to compensate. If processing reduces solubility (rare), grind finer.

### Determinant 3: Origin & Altitude (Bean Density)

Denser beans are harder to extract. Altitude = density = solubility:

| Factor | Grind Adjustment | Why |
|--------|-----------------|-----|
| **High altitude (1200m+)** | Finer (+1-2 clicks) | Dense cells, slower internal water penetration |
| **Medium altitude (800-1200m)** | Base | Standard density |
| **Low altitude (<800m)** | Coarser (+1 click) | Less dense, water penetrates faster |
| **Panama (Gesha)** | Finer (+1-2 clicks) | Gesha variety is notably dense; known for tight extraction windows |
| **Ethiopia Yirgacheffe** | Base to finer | High-grown heirloom varieties tend to be dense |
| **Brazil (low altitude)** | Coarser (+1-2 clicks) | Brazilian Cerrado is often lower altitude, softer beans |

**Note:** Elevation data from the roaster/lot is more useful than country alone. When in doubt, denser-origin coffees (high-grown, volcanic soil, Gesha) benefit from going finer.

### Determinant 4: Roast Level (Bean Hardness & Porosity)

Roast level changes bean structure, which changes extraction behavior:

| Roast Level | Density | Porosity | Grind Adjustment |
|-------------|---------|----------|-----------------|
| **Light** | Dense (hard) | Low (less porous) | Finer (+1-2 clicks) — harder for water to penetrate; need more surface area |
| **Medium** | Medium | Medium | Base setting |
| **Medium-Dark** | Less dense | More porous | Coarser (+1 click) — water enters easily; too fine = over-extraction fast |
| **Dark** | Least dense (soft, brittle) | Most porous | Coarser still — extracts very quickly; dark roast bitterness compounds fast |

**The paradox:** Light roasts feel "fresher" and brighter but need *finer* grind to extract enough. Dark roasts taste "stronger" but need *coarser* grind to avoid bitter over-extraction.

### Determinant 5: Variety (Bean Size & Structure)

Large beans have different particle size distributions and extract differently:

| Variety | Grind Adjustment | Why |
|---------|-----------------|-----|
| **Pacamara / Maragogipe** | Base +1 click | Large beans (inherited from Maragogipe). Particle distribution differs; standard grind settings can produce uneven extraction. Expect slower drawdown. |
| **Gesha / Geisha** | Base to -1 click | Dense, small-leafed variety; known for tight extraction windows. On the finer side of medium-fine. |
| **Typica / Bourbon** | Base | Standard — these are the genetic parents of most coffee varieties |
| **SL28 / SL34 (Kenya)** | Base | Dense, handles higher temps; standard grind behavior |
| **Caturra / Cataui** | Base | Compact plant, standard bean size |
| **Heirloom (Ethiopia)** | Base to -1 click | Genetic diversity means wide variation; typically dense, err finer |

## Stacking Adjustments: How to Combine Determinants

Grind adjustments are **cumulative and often competing**. A natural Gesha from Panama (high altitude, light roast) and a Brazilian natural (low altitude, dark roast) might need the *same* brew method but completely different grind settings — in opposite directions.

**Decision hierarchy:**

1. **Start with brew method base** (from Determinant 1 table)
2. **Apply processing adjustment** (from Determinant 2 table) — this is the biggest swing factor
3. **Apply origin/altitude** (from Determinant 3 table)
4. **Apply roast level** (from Determinant 4 table)
5. **Apply variety** (from Determinant 5 table)

**Example — Your El Salvador Gesha Natural (1200+ msnm, light-medium roast):**

| Step | Factor | Adjustment | Logic |
|------|--------|-----------|-------|
| 1 | V60 base | Medium-fine | Base brew method |
| 2 | Natural process | +1-2 clicks (coarser) | More soluble = need to slow extraction |
| 3 | High altitude (1200m+) | -1 click (finer) | Dense beans = harder to extract |
| 4 | Light-medium roast | Base to -1 | Light roast = denser, needs help extracting |
| 5 | Gesha variety | -1 click | Dense, tight window |

**Net result:** ~0.6.0 to 0.7.5 on K-Ultra — medium-fine side, but coarser than the absolute finest edge thanks to the natural process.

**Example — Brazilian Natural, Medium-Dark, 800m:**

| Step | Factor | Adjustment | Logic |
|------|--------|-----------|-------|
| 1 | V60 base | Medium-fine | Base brew method |
| 2 | Natural process | +1-2 clicks (coarser) | More soluble |
| 3 | Low altitude | +1 click (coarser) | Softer, easier extraction |
| 4 | Medium-dark roast | +1 click (coarser) | Porous, fast extraction |
| 5 | Bourbon variety | Base | Standard |

**Net result:** ~0.8.5 to 0.9.5 on K-Ultra — near the medium-coarse range, noticeably coarser than the Gesha and accounting for the natural process + darker roast.

## Quick Reference: Grind Adjustment Cheat Sheet

Use this when you need a quick decision rather than walking through all 5 determinants:

| If your coffee is... | Grind direction | Reason |
|---------------------|----------------|--------|
| Natural process | **Coarser** | More soluble, extracts faster |
| Honey (red/black) | Slightly coarser | Higher sugar content, more soluble |
| Washed | Base or finer | Standard solubility |
| Anaerobic / Experimental | **Coarser** | Much more soluble |
| High altitude (1200m+) | **Finer** | Denser, harder to extract |
| Low altitude (<800m) | Coarser | Softer, easier extraction |
| Light roast | **Finer** | Dense, less porous |
| Dark roast | Coarser | Porous, extracts fast |
| Large bean (Pacamara/Maragogipe) | Slightly coarser | Different particle distribution |
| Gesha variety | Finer (tight window) | Dense, delicate |

## Common Mistakes That Produce Wrong Grind Settings

| Mistake | Result | Fix |
|---------|--------|-----|
| Setting grind purely from brew method, ignoring process | Natural coffee over-extracts (bitter + sour simultaneously) | Add +1-3 clicks coarser for naturals |
| Using "espresso fine" thinking for all Gesha | Gesha extracts too fast, loses florals | Gesha needs medium-fine, not fine |
| Going finer to "get more out of light roasts" without considering altitude | Under-extraction if altitude wasn't accounted for | High-altitude light roasts need finer, but process still dominates adjustment |
| Assuming darker roast = need finer grind | Dark roast bitter/astringent | Dark roasts are already porous — go coarser |
| Using same grind for Pacamara as for Caturra | Uneven extraction, unpredictable drawdown | Large beans need slight coarser adjustment |
| Using standard AeroPress grind for inverted/long-immersion (2+ min, 20 g+) | Over-extraction: bitter, astringent, muddy | Go coarser by ~10 ticks (K-Ultra) or 2-4 steps (Encore ESP). Use HCG steep-and-release range, not standard AeroPress range. |

## When in Doubt

- **For a new coffee, always start coarser** than you think you need. Under-extraction (sour) is easier to fix than over-extraction (bitter). You can grind finer on subsequent brews.
- **If you can't decide direction**, trust processing first, then altitude. These two factors have the largest swing on grind settings.
- **Mark your settings** — always note: grind setting, coffee name, brew method, process, and outcome. This builds your personal dial-in baseline over time.
